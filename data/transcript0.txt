I am often asked, usually at the beginning of a lecture, if I can recommend a book that covers the lecture material. Since I usually can't give a short answer to that, I thought I'd make a video about it, and here it is. From my perspective, books on software technology can be divided into two categories. The first category includes really good books that you don't necessarily need to own, because there are libraries, online sources, and many other ways to access knowledge. These books in the first category can be divided into five classes. I borrowed this classification from Johannes Siedersleben, which I'll explain in a moment. The first category is my own idea. The first class includes the software technology classics, which are always mentioned when any software technology standards are referenced. These are useful for a bachelor's degree and similar studies. Then there's the area of theory, academic theory, i.e., what comes from academic and university research. Then there are books about patterns, styles, and design patterns. Next, there are technical books, and finally, there are books offering good advice. Let's start with the software technology classics. The first one to mention is Helmut Balzert's textbook on software technology. This is considered the standard work, at least in the German-speaking world of software technology. Comparable to this is Ian Somerville's book on software engineering, in English. Then there are many other books, such as the aforementioned Johannes Siedersleben's work, which focuses more on specific areas of software engineering, especially architectural themes. There are many books of this kind. Siedersleben is also a well-known author who greatly influenced SD&M. SD&M is a consulting company that was acquired by Capgemini and has made significant contributions to software engineering, particularly in process models. I would also include the numerous UML guides in this category of books, of which I have given an example. These books have one thing in common: they are very thick. Balzert and Somerville both have around 800 pages. Siedersleben's book is not quite as thick, but still several hundred pages. The UML guides vary in thickness. These books are comprehensive, covering almost everything you can imagine about software engineering. Basically, everything you never dared to ask about software engineering, which is included in the standards, can be found in these books. This extensive coverage is both a strength and a weakness of these books. They provide a comprehensive overview of all aspects of software engineering, covering all areas of the discipline. However, they can be somewhat unstructured. So if you need to build an application or a software system and are wondering how to proceed,or a software system and are wondering how to proceed, let's say you are in a small company with 1, 2, 3, or 4 colleagues working as a team, you won't find practical guidance in these books. They are simply not designed for that. They operate at a much higher level, discussing processes, life cycles, and process models, but they don't provide much practical advice on how to actually create software. That's why I think these books are useful as reference works. They are helpful if you want to look up something to classify it in a professional context. For example, if you need to comply with certain standards, then I would always consult such a guide to see which area of software technology it pertains to. I strongly suspect that these books are mainly used as reference works rather than being read from cover to cover. They are more likely to be consulted as needed. Then there's the area of academic theory, which is, unfortunately, relatively sparse. Software technology is one of the few current industrial fields where, at least it seems to me, almost all innovations do not come from academic research. If you look at where innovations in software development have emerged in recent decades, you'll find a vibrant culture of people who are influential through textbooks, blogs, lectures, and have made a name for themselves this way. You will find fewer articles based on research projects, dissertations, or other academic sources. To avoid misunderstanding, I am talking about software development. In many other IT areas, this is different. Academic researchers are definitely innovators in those fields. But in software technology, it doesn't seem to be the case. At least, I don't know of any work that has significantly influenced current developments in software technology and should be mentioned here with a university origin. Now we come to the third important category, the category of patterns. Of course, we must mention the original book on patterns, the so-called Gang of Four book by Erich Gamma and colleagues. Design Patterns, a book that is decades old, still relevant, still worth reading, and still worth buying. There are many other books on this topic. For example, I once introduced Joachim Goll's book, Architecture and Design Patterns of Software Technology. I know the book, I like it very much, and find it very useful. I would also say that these books are primarily reference works that you can have, but probably don't necessarily need to own, because you can learn a lot about software design patterns through online research and many online sources where such patterns are well documented. This is now a very widespread and accepted style in software technology, so certain structures have been established, and at least in the fundamental areas, not much new has happened in recent years. Then we have the fourth area, the area of technical books. This is probably the most dynamic area, whether you want to know something about Java, for example, the innovations in versions 9 to 14. I chose this book randomly; I don't know it myself. Or whether you want to read something about Spring Boot, or if you are interested in the programming language Rust, which is known for its small memory footprint and high runtime efficiency, making it recommended for embedded environments as an alternative to C. Or one of the many other technical topics currently available in IT, of which maybe three-quarters will be less interesting in five years. These books are absolutely useful, necessary, and generally have a fairly short half-life. Successful books are updated regularly in new editions and thus remain current. The books that don't get updated tend to disappear, either because they weren't successful from the start or because they become outdated. These are books you would probably buy if you are diving into a new topic, getting deeply involved in a new project, or dealing with a new programming language or framework and want to learn it properly. In such cases, you will certainly buy one, two, or three books on the subject. But I strongly suspect that you will read these books from start to finish, but at some point, you will put them aside and probably refer to them again from time to time if something is unclear. And here, too, it often happens that with many topics, especially once you've worked on them a bit, you don't really need the book anymore but rather look at Stack Overflow or other online sources. Finally, there's the fifth category, which consists of the so-called good tips. I've chosen two exemplary ones that I think are very good. One is from Stefan Zörner, "Documenting and Communicating Software Architectures." Stefan Zörner has focused on this aspect of software development in particular. He assumes that a software system is already in place, that it has been created, and that there are architectural decisions that need to be communicated. He gives hints on what to look out for when doing that. The book for software architects is by Peter Ruschka and Gernot Starke. They have also written a number of other similar books together and individually. Ruschka and Starke are the two behind the ARC42 project, which is the most widely used template for architecture documentation in Germany, and they are sought-after speakers when it comes to architecture. This book is also typical of this category of books, i.e., a compendium of tips and tricks based on a certain section of the entire software technology. These are books that you can definitely have, so I have them both, and that you occasionally need to refer to, especially if you are dealing with, for example, how to set up the documentation for a certain project. But they have more of a reference character, at least that's how I feel. So I also read these books through once at the beginning, skipping certain chapters that didn't seem that exciting to me, so I didn't necessarily read them all, but at least a large part of them.
